Assistive Belt

Wearable walking aid technology for visually impaired

Introduction

What is this about

It has been estimated that globally, 40 to 45 million people are totally blind, 135 million have low vision and 314 million have some kind of visual impairment. The motivation brhind this product is to provide technological and economical alternative to the walking sticks, the visually impaired use and give them greater degree of independence. The idea here is to develop a safety device that can be worn by the visually challenged and which will help them in their daily chores giving greater degree of freedom.

Approach

Steps taken to build the product

  • User interviews were done to understand the pain point they were facing with the conventional use of walking stick.
  • Deciding on what technology to use in order to cater to the core functionality of the product which is obstacle detection.
  • After running SWOT analysis of various sensor technologies out there we finalized on ultrasonic sensors.
  • We decided to go with a wearble device and considered various forms ranging from hand gloves to belt to shoes and finalized on belt as it is optimally located to detect obstacles.
  • The number of sensor modules and position of these on the belt were determined using Anthropometric data. In total 6 ultrasonic sensors are used at 3 positions on the belt which gives 120 degree field of detection.
  • Each sensor module also contains a 3V micro vibrator which gives the feedback of obstacle presence as vibrations to the user.
  • It is designed to detect obstacles at approximately 2 step distance from the user.
  • Nylon fabric (Kodra) material is used to make the belt since it is abrasion resistant and relatively light weight compared to canvas materials.
  • The belt can withhold a person's waist size of up to 50 inches.
  • Sensor modules are mounted on a sliding pounch in order to adjust the position according to the person's waist.
  • Finally, small design changes were incorporated by taking continuos feedback from the blind participants.

Final product

Functionality and the look and feel

The idea here is to develop an easy to wear and light weight wearable device that could potentially replace the use of walking sticks and help the visually challenged in navigating hands-free. In the initial iteration we used only 4 sensors and placed them at 2 locations on the belt. After testing the belt with the users we later modified our design to accomodate 6 sensors at 3 different positions on the belt in order to give greater accuracy of detection. Also, we made these module positions adjustable so as to cater to people of all waist sizes.
